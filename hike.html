The Hike

Reintal Valley Route

The hike from Garmisch-Partenkirchen Skistadion up Reintal Valley to the top of Zugspitze is both awe-inspiring and challenging. The Reintal Valley route is considered safe and not technically demanding - suitable for any fit hiker. But it is rated moderate to hard difficulty due to its length 21.7km (13.5mi) and elevation gain 2,200m (7,218ft). Additionally,

The first section of trail passes through the picturesque Partnach Gorge followed by a long gentle ascent through the scenic Reintal Valley. After passing the Reintalangerhütte (9mi) the trail sharply ascends past treeline into rocky alpine terrain. About 1km past the Knorrhütte (11mi), the trail begins to flatten out and alternate between a more gentle uphill and flatter sections.

At the Sonnalpin Station (12.5mi), hikers may opt to ride the tram the rest of the way up, or if enough time and leg stamina remain, continue by foot. The final section continues up the large scree field and crosses onto a more solid but more exposed rocky section with anchored cables as safety handles, requiring increased caution if snow is present. This scree field and rocky section is the hardest part, but hikers who persist are rewarded with spectacular views.

For more information about the Reintal Valley route, see some links from Der Eskapist, Bavaria.travel, Triptins.com, and Strava.

Additional Notes

  • For early hikers, cheap overnight parking is available at the Garmisch-Partenkirchen Skistadion. For anyone departing later in the day, it is recommended to take public transit to the trailhead. Alternatively, it may also be possible to walk direct from your lodging in Garmisch-Partenkirchen.
  • Cellular signal along the trail is not always present and sometimes completely absent, so hikers should review relevant maps and details in advance beforehand.
  • In the early morning the Partnach Gorge is closed, and hikers will need to bypass it. The easiest detour is to take the stairs next to the ticket turnstile, then at the top of the Gorge, cross the Eiserne (metal structure) bridge and follow the signs to the right towards Kaiserschmarrn Alm. The trail rejoins at the river fork.
  • Hikers need to be aware of when the "last tram" is. In the summer the last tram may be at 1730, but off-season it may be at 1630. Additionally, both German and Austrian Zugspitze trams close for maintenance several weeks out of the year (at different times usually). The other option is to catch the smaller tram down to Sonnalpin then the Zugspitzebahn Cogwheel train back to Garmisch, if its not closed too. Check the schedule before you go. See here and here
  • Occasionally the Tyrolean (Austrian) Zugspitze tram offers special early or late hours for certain events. Planners should confirm the cost of a 1-way ticket or if it is even possible to catch it back down (into Austria) on these occasions. See here and here.

Gatterl and Other Routes

The Gatterl route is a shorter alternative to the Reintal Valley route, but may require additional planning for several reasons. First, the route covers more elevation which means steeper trails and potential for dangerous terrain. Second, the Gatterl route begins in Hof, Austria, which means planning transport and parking, or relying upon public transit. Finally, the Austrian side sells a discounted ticket specifically for the Gatterl route, which includes one ascent on the Ehrwalder Almbahn gondola, and one descent on the Tiroler (Austrian) Zugspitze tram into Obermoos, Austria, which may necessitate additional travel planning.

Other (more advanced) routes not covered on this webpage include the Höllental route (via ferrata and glacier crossing requiring alpine equipment) from Hammersbach, Germany, and Stopselzieher (via ferrata) from Obermoos, Austria.
