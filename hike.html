The Hike

Reintal Valley Route

Distance: 13.5 mi (21.7 km)    Climb: 7,200 ft (2,200 m)    Time: 10 hrs.

The hike from Garmisch-Partenkirchen Skistadion up Reintal Valley to the Germany's highest mountain peak, the Zugspitze, is both awe-inspiring and challenging. The trail is safe and suitable for any fit hiker - no technical skills or special equipment is needed. It's difficulty lies in its length and elevation gain, most of which occurs towards the end of the hike.

The route first passes through the impressive Partnach Gorge natural monument. It gently ascends with the Partnach river up the scenic Reintal valley south, then west. About 1 km past the Reintalangerhütte (9 mi), the trail ascends steeply past treeline into rockier alpine terrain. After the Knorrhütte (11 mi), the trail slowly begins to flatten out and alternate between more gentle uphills and stretches of flat ground, and passes through several snowfields. At Sonnalpin Station (12.5 mi) hikers may ride the tram the rest of the way up, or continue on foot if enough time remains.

The last stretch of the trail continues up the large scree field and crosses onto a more solid but exposed rocky section with anchored cables as safety handles. This is considered a low-grade via ferrata, requiring no additional gear, but more caution if snow is present. The scree field and rocky section are the steepest parts, requiring more leg stamina and a head for heights, but in return hikers are granted spectacular views from along the ridge. At the very top are more panoramic views, restaurants, and access to the summit cross, which can involve waiting in a queue on nicer days.

For reviews and more information about this hike, see Der-Eskapist, Bavaria.travel, Triptins.com, and Strava.

Notes

  • There is no cellular signal along portions of the trail.
  • Overnight parking is available at the Skistadion (prepay using the machines), as well as at the (more expensive) Zugspitze tram base parking lot.
  • In the early morning when the Partnach Gorge is closed, it must be bypassed by going up and over. The shortest bypass is to take the stairs immediately left of the ticket turnstile, cross overhead and continue up the trail. Higher up at the fork, cross the gorge using the bridge to your left. At the next intersection follow the signs to the right towards Kaiserschmarrn Alm, and continue past the Alm and down again. The trail rejoins at the river fork.
  • Hikers need to be aware of when the "last tram down" is. In the summer the last tram may be at 1730, but off-season it may be at 1630. Additionally, both German and Austrian Zugspitze trams close for maintenance several weeks out of the year. A third option is to catch the smaller Gletscherbahn tram down to Sonnalpin then take the Zugspitzebahn Cogwheel train back to Garmisch, if it's not closed too. Schedules are usually posted to the websites, as of early 2026 are here and here.
  • In-season, during regular business hours, both of the huts mentioned below, and also at the Bockhütte (6 mi) there are cafes where hikers can stop and order something to eat or drink. Plan to pay with cash.

The Huts

Mountain huts along the trail allow hikers to conquer the mountain in two days instead of one, with several benefits. Hikers don't have to walk the first several miles in complete darkness, and can enjoy the views of the lower Reintal valley. They don't have to bypass the Partnach Gorge, which is closed in the early morning. They don't have to worry about making it to the top in time to catch the last tram down. Additionally, if good weather is predicted for the second day, staying overnight allows the group to make an early start for the summit, potentially avoiding some afternoon crowds. One notable disadvantage is that staying overnight usually means carrying extra gear, such as a towel, slippers and toiletries.

Reintalangerhütte

Info & Booking

The Reintalangerhütte is below treeline, and about 9 miles along the trail. It sits in front of the Partnach river, and is nearby some grassy meadows in the upper Reintal Valley. According to their website, they no longer have showers, but there are places to take a quick cold dip in the Partnach river.

Knorrhütte

Info & Booking

The Knorrhütte is above treeline, surrounded by rocky alpine karst terrain. It sits move than halfway up the main uphill section of trail, at about 11 miles along. It's 2 miles further and over 2,000 feet higher than the Reintalangerhütte, but also that much closer to the top. Per their website they have pay-to-use showers.

Notes

  • Both huts listed above are managed by the German Alpine Club (DAV) with some unique rules. One rule states all boots must be left in a special room - guests must change into slippers or sandals inside to keep the floor clean. Another rule states guests are prohibited from using regular sleeping bags. They must instead bring a special hüttenschlafsack (sleeping bag liner without insulation) for hygenic reasons. These usually sell for around €25 in outdoor stores, or ask around someone might have extras to lend.
  • Overnight stay at either hut costs €35 to €45 per person depending upon room type, and €45 to €50 per person for half-board which includes dinner and breakfast. You select if you want the half-board when you make the reservation. (In 2025 the half-board at Reintalangerhütte was pretty good and the atmosphere nice.)
  • Reservations are managed through separate but easy-to-use online reservation systems that usually open beginning in December the previous year, thus many beds get booked far in advance. However, it's free to cancel up until a week before the booking date, (after which cancellation fees begin to rise), so keep checking for vacancies, particular within 7-10 days of your preferred dates.
  • To make a reservation online and for any applicable late-cancellation fees, the system accepts credit card. But when you arrive in person at the hut on the day of your booking, they require you to pay the full reservation amount in Euros.
  • If you can't make your booking and it's too late to cancel online, call the hut directly to cancel using the number on their website. Otherwise, you will be charged and the hut staff could report you as a no-show, which could trigger an unnecessary search or emergency response.

Weather

The weather makes such a big difference on this hike, that it may not even be worth going if snowfall or poor weather is forecasted. Webcams can give a good peek at what trail conditions are like at altitude.

Additional Notes

  • For additional lodging options closer to the Garmisch-Partenkirchen Skistadion, check out this Airbnb and this fancy hotel. More camping and lodging options are available in Garmisch-Partenkirchen.
  • Occasionally the Austrian tram to Zugspitze opens early or stays open late for special events (assuming you can commute to/from Austria or wherever you left your car). Hikers should confirm the cost and availability of a 1-way ticket on these dates, before committing to this plan. See here and here.
  • More technical routes not discussed on this page include the Höllental route (via ferrata and glacier crossing requiring alpine equipment) from Hammersbach, Germany, and Stopselzieher (via ferrata) from Obermoos, Austria.
  • The German Alpine Club (DAV)'s advertises the official Zugspitze Tour as a 3-day hike. The first night is spent in the Reintalangerhütte. Hikers climb the Zugspitze on the second day, and then stay the second night in the Knorrhütte. Then on the third day they descend via the Gatterl, into Ehrwald, Austria.
  • For anyone interested in ascending Zugspitze via the Gatterl route, it may be shorter distance, but it's still a serious hike at 9 mi (14.3 km) long and up to 6,400 ft (1,950 ) uphill. Like the Reintal valley route, for enough time to reach the top, hikers would probably have to begin around 0430, or 0830 if staying overnight at the Knorrhütte. More information here.
