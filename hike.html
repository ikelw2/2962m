The Hike

Reintal Valley Route

The hike from Garmisch-Partenkirchen Skistadion up Reintal Valley to the top of Zugspitze is both awe-inspiring and challenging. The route is considered safe and not technically demanding - suitable for any fit hiker. But it is rated moderate to difficult due to its length 21.7km (13.5mi) and elevation gain 2,200m (7,218ft). Most of the elevation gain occurs in the last one-third of the hike.

The route first passes through the impressive Partnach Gorge natural monument. It gently ascends with the Partnach river south, then west, up the scenic Reintal valley. About 1km past the Reintalangerhütte (9mi), the trail ascends steeply past treeline into rockier alpine terrain. After the Knorrhütte (11mi), the trail slowly begins to flatten out and alternate between more gentle uphills and stretches of flat ground, likely still covered in snow. At Sonnalpin Station (12.5mi), hikers may opt to ride the tram the rest of the way, or continue on foot if enough time and leg stamina remain.

The last stretch of the trail continues up the large scree field and crosses onto a more solid but exposed rocky section with anchored cables as safety handles. This is considered a low-grade via ferrata, requiring no additional gear, but more caution if snow is present. The scree field and rocky section is the hardest part, physically and perhaps psychologically as well. Hikers who persist are rewarded with spectacular views from the rocky section and ridge. At the top there are more panoramic views, restaurants, and a short climb to the summit cross.

For more information about the Reintal Valley route, see der-eskapist, bavaria.travel, triptins, strava, and atlas.bayern.

Gatterl and Other Routes

The German Alpine Club (DAV)'s markets the official Zugspitze Tour as a 3-day hike. The first night is spent in the Reintalangerhütte. Hikers climb the Zugspitze on the second day, and then stay the second night in the Knorrhütte. Then on the third day they descend via the Gatterl, into Ehrwald, Austria.

For anyone interested in ascending Zugspitze in one day via the Gatterl route, it may be shorter, but it's still a serious hike at 14.3km (9mi) long and up to 1,950m (6,400ft) uphill. More information here.

Other (more advanced) routes not covered on this site include the Höllental route (via ferrata and glacier crossing requiring alpine equipment) from Hammersbach, Germany, and Stopselzieher (via ferrata) from Obermoos, Austria.

The Hüttes

Mountain huts along the trail allow hikers to conquer the mountain in two days instead of one, with several added benefits. Hikers don't have to walk the first several miles in complete darkness, and can enjoy the views of the lower Reintal valley. They don't have to bypass the Partnach Gorge, which is closed in the early morning. They don't have to worry about making it to the top in time to catch the last tram down. Additionally, if good weather is predicted for the second day, staying overnight allows the group to make an early start for the summit, potentially avoiding the late-afternoon crowds. One notable disadvantage is that staying overnight usually means having to carry extra gear with you, such as a small towel, soap and toiletries.

Both huts discussed below are managed by the German Alpine Club (DAV) with some special rules. One rule is all boots must be left in a special room - guests must wear slippers or sandals inside to keep the living areas clean. Another rule is that guests are prohibited from using regular sleeping bags. They must instead bring a special hüttenschlafsack (sleeping bag liner without insulation) for hygenic reasons. Ask around someone might have one to borrow, or you can purchase one at outdoor stores for around €25.

Overnight stay at either hut costs €35 to €45 per person depending upon room type, and another €45 to €50 per person for half-board - dinner and breakfast. (In 2025 the half-board at Reintalangerhütte was tasty and atmosphere nice.) Reservations are managed through separate but easy-to-use online systems that usually open in December, thus many beds are booked early. However, cancellations are free until about week before the booking (then they start increasing over time), so keep checking for available spots, particular within 7-10 days of your preferred dates. Finally, if you can't make your booking and can't cancel online, contact the hut directly to cancel. Otherwise, you may be charged and the hut staff could report you as a no-show, which could trigger an unnecessary search or emergency response.

Reintalangerhütte

The Reintalangerhütte is below treeline, and about 9 miles along the trail. It sits in front of the Partnach river, and is nearby some grassy meadows in the upper Reintal Valley. Per their website, they no longer have a shower. More information and reservation link here.

Knorrhütte

The Knorrhütte is above treeline, surrounded by rocky alpine karst terrain. It sits move than halfway up the main uphill section of trail, at about 11 miles along. It's 2 miles further and over 2,000 feet higher than the Reintalangerhütte, but also that much closer to the top. There are pay-to-use showers. More information and reservation link here.

Additional Considerations

  • There is no cellular signal along several sections of trail.
  • For early hikers, cheap overnight parking is available at the Garmisch-Partenkirchen Skistadion lot here.
  • In the early morning when the Partnach Gorge is closed, it must be bypassed. Take the stairs left of the turnstile, at the fork go left over the bridge, follow the signs to the right past the Kaiserschmarrn Alm. The trail rejoins at the river fork.
  • Hikers need to be aware of when the "last tram down" is. In the summer the last tram may be at 1730, but off-season it may be at 1630. Additionally, both German and Austrian Zugspitze trams close for maintenance several weeks out of the year. Another option is to catch the smaller Gletscherbahn tram down to Sonnalpin then the Zugspitzebahn Cogwheel train back to Garmisch, if it's not closed too. Schedules are usually posted to the websites, as of Feb 2026 are here and here
  • Occasionally the Austrian Zugspitze tram offers special early or late hours for certain events. Hikers should confirm the cost and availability of a 1-way ticket down on these dates. See here and here.

Weather

Snow and weather conditions determine if the hike can be completed, especially in the off-season. If the conditions are bad enough, local guides have even been known to force hikers to turn back. Check these links when planning.
